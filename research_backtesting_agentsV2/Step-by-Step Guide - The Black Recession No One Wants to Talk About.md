## What This Guide Covers

Key economic data points and structural analysis from Shari Dunn on disproportionate Black unemployment, anti-DEI employment impacts, and implications for economic indicator reliability.

---

## Step 1: Understand the Unemployment Gap

1. Black unemployment: ~8% overall, 7.4% for Black women
2. White unemployment: ~4.4% overall, ~3.5% for white women
3. Historical pattern: Black unemployment consistently tracks at **2x** the white rate
4. If overall economy hit 7-8%, it would be declared a recession

---

## Step 2: Recognize Anti-DEI Employment Impact

1. Anti-DEI rhetoric directly correlates with rising Black unemployment
2. Historical parallel: during COVID, anti-Asian rhetoric spiked Asian-American unemployment from ~2% to 8%
3. Current anti-DEI environment producing an analogous effect on Black employment

---

## Step 3: Question Official Economic Data

1. Bureau of Labor Statistics official was fired for reporting accurate job numbers
2. Net job growth since inauguration: **-92,000 jobs**
3. Official figures may understate true unemployment
4. Verify economic data against multiple independent sources

---

## Step 4: Assess Macro-Economic Implications

1. Compounding bias (race + gender + age) creates outsized economic impact on Black families
2. Black women as equal economic partners means income loss has proportionally greater household impact
3. Consequences ripple through: 401(k) withdrawals, public assistance dependency, consumer spending reduction
4. These structural factors affect consumer spending data and economic growth projections

---

## Key Takeaway

> A hidden recession is occurring within Black communities driven by anti-DEI rhetoric and structural discrimination. The negative job growth figures and compromised BLS data reliability suggest official economic indicators may be painting an overly optimistic picture — relevant context for anyone analyzing US macro-economic conditions.
