## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The most valuable AI skill is not prompting or workflow design — it is the ability to **reject bad AI output** and articulate precisely why it is wrong. Nate B Jones argues that "rejection" is a systematically undervalued competency that, if captured and encoded as structured constraints, becomes a compounding institutional asset. He references OpenAI's GDPval benchmark (frontier models beat 14-year professionals 70% of the time at <1% cost) and argues the critical question is: who determines quality for both the 70% that passes AND the 30% that fails?

## KEY TOPICS

### The 70/30 Split (OpenAI GDPval Benchmark)
- Frontier models beat or tie professionals averaging 14 years of experience 70% of the time
- 100x faster at less than 1% of the cost
- Tasks created and graded by those professionals in double-blind evaluation
- 5 rounds of expert review per task — each a rejection event

### Three Dimensions of Rejection as a Competency

| Dimension | Definition | Example |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Recognition** | Detecting something is wrong | Depends on deep domain experience; junior analysts miss flawed regulatory assumptions |
| **Articulation** | Explaining WHY it's wrong as a usable constraint | "This isn't right because you're treating all requirements identically" |
| **Encoding** | Making that constraint persist beyond the moment | Structured database, not Slack threads that evaporate |

### The Rejection Flywheel
1. AI generates output
2. Expert rejects with articulated reason
3. Rejection gets encoded as structured constraint
4. Constraint library grows
5. Each cycle raises the taste bar
6. Ratio of expert-hours to encoded constraints improves every cycle

### Real-World Examples
- **Epic Systems** — Won healthcare by encoding clinical workflows from thousands of hospitals (300M+ patient records). Moat is encoded judgment, not software.
- **Bloomberg** — Same pattern in financial data
- **Vertical SaaS** — Any company that truly owns a niche does some version of this

### Practical Solution: Constraint Library via MCP Server
- MCP server backed by a database
- Captures rejections as structured constraints without leaving the chat tool
- Zero context switching — capture happens as side effect of the rejection already being performed
- Tool available on speaker's Substack

## ACTIONABLE TAKEAWAYS

1. **Start tracking rejection patterns** — Log what you say no to and why
2. **Build a constraint library** — MCP server + database, or even a structured document
3. **Stop letting rejections fall on the floor** — Most constraints currently evaporate in email/Slack
4. **For hiring:** Value recognition skill (domain depth) over prompting skill (tool fluency)
5. **For teams:** Challenge members to explain WHY they rejected AI output — this builds shared quality understanding
