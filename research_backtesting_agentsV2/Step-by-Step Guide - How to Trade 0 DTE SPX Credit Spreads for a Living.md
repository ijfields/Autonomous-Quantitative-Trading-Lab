## The MAX Framework

The speaker uses a 4-step framework called **MAX(S)** for finding high-probability 0DTE credit spread trades:

| Letter | Step | Description |
|--------|------|-------------|
| **M** | Market Trend | Identify long-term and short-term trend direction |
| **A** | Area of Interest | Find support/resistance levels with higher-than-average implied volatility |
| **C** | Criteria of Entry | Wait for candlestick confirmation (hammer, engulfing, break of level) |
| **S** | Stop-Loss | Set stop slightly below/above the confirming candle formation |

## Part 1: Market Trend Analysis (M)

### Step 1: Identify the Long-Term Trend
Open the daily chart of SPX the evening before the trade day.

- Look for the classic trend structure: higher highs and higher lows (uptrend) or lower highs and lower lows (downtrend)
- In the example trade (October 23rd), the daily chart showed an obvious uptrend: low → high → higher low → higher high → higher low → higher high
- Expected result: Clear directional bias (bullish, bearish, or sideways)

### Step 2: Identify the Short-Term Trend
Drop down to the **30-minute chart** to assess intraday momentum.

- Look for the same trend structure at this shorter timeframe
- In the example, the 30-minute chart showed the market **consolidating/sideways** — going from low to high to the same high, then a lower low, then a higher low, then back to the same level
- The combination of long-term up + short-term sideways tells you to look for **bullish trades at support**
- Expected result: Understanding of whether the short-term trend aligns with or diverges from the long-term trend

---

## Part 2: Finding Areas of Interest (A)

### Step 3: Draw Support and Resistance on the Higher Timeframe
Return to the daily or 30-minute chart and mark key levels.

- **Support levels:** Previous swing lows that acted as launching points for new highs. In the example, 5770-5780 was identified as support because it was a previous swing low that preceded a breakout higher
- **Resistance levels:** Previous swing highs. In the example, 5878 was the last significant high
- Draw horizontal lines at these levels
- Expected result: 2-3 clearly defined support/resistance zones on your chart

### Step 4: Confirm Areas with Implied Volatility
Check that your identified areas align with implied volatility spikes.

- Areas of support and resistance typically have higher-than-average implied volatility
- When price enters these zones and IV spikes, it signals the market recognizes the level as important
- This creates better credit for your spread (higher IV = higher premiums)
- Expected result: Your support/resistance areas coincide with IV spikes on the implied volatility chart

---

## Part 3: Criteria of Entry (C)

### Step 5: Wait for Candlestick Confirmation
Do NOT enter just because price reaches your area — wait for confirmation.

- **For bullish trades (put credit spreads):**
  - Look for a **hammer candle** at support (long lower wick, small body)
  - Then wait for the **next candle** to break above the high of the hammer
  - In the example: At 11:00 AM, a hammer formed at 5775. At 11:30, the next candle broke above the hammer's high → entry signal

- **For bearish trades (call credit spreads):**
  - Look for a **break below support** confirming a new downtrend
  - In the missed morning trade example: Price broke below the consolidation support, closing below it → entry signal for a bearish trade

- Expected result: A clear, rules-based entry trigger — not a guess

### Step 6: Place the Credit Spread
Once confirmed, enter the trade aligned with your directional bias.

- **Bullish confirmation → sell a put credit spread:**
  - Sell the put at/near the money (at the support level)
  - Buy the protective put 5 points below
  - Example: Sold 5775/5770 put credit spread × 6 contracts = $155 credit each = $930 total

- **Bearish confirmation → sell a call credit spread:**
  - Sell the call at/near the money (at the broken support, now resistance)
  - Buy the protective call 5 points above

- Expected result: Position entered with defined max profit and max loss

---

## Part 4: Stop-Loss Management (S)

### Step 7: Set Your Stop-Loss
Place your stop slightly beyond the confirming candle formation.

- **For bullish trades:** Set stop slightly below the hammer candle's low
  - Example: Hammer low was ~5772, stop set at 5770
  - If price retraces back to 5770, exit — the bullish thesis is invalidated

- **For bearish trades:** Set stop slightly above the breakdown candle
  - Example: Breakdown candle high was ~5821, stop set at 5824

- **Risk/Reward math:** Losing ~$50 per spread vs. making ~$155 per spread = roughly 1:3 risk-to-reward ratio
- Expected result: Disciplined exit plan set before the trade plays out

## Common Pitfalls
- **Trading against the trend:** The long-term trend should be your primary directional guide. Only trade short-term counter-trend moves if the area of interest is very strong.
- **Entering without confirmation:** Price touching a support/resistance area is NOT enough — wait for the candlestick pattern to confirm.
- **No stop-loss:** Always define your stop before entering. If the confirming candle is invalidated, exit immediately.
- **Ignoring implied volatility:** Areas without elevated IV give you less premium and may not hold as significant levels.
- **Over-sizing:** Max loss should be an amount you can comfortably absorb. Start with 1-2 contracts and scale up.
