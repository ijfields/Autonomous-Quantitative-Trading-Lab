## Common Pitfalls
- **Chasing yield:** The highest-yielding stocks (15-21%) often had the worst total returns. High yield can signal capital erosion.
- **Ignoring total return:** Focusing only on dividend income misses the picture — a stock paying great dividends while losing 40% of its value is a net loss.
- **Short-term bias:** 1-year results can be misleading. Permian Basin looked terrible in 2023 but was the #1 performer over 5 years.
- **Assuming monthly = better:** Monthly dividends are convenient but don't inherently make a stock a good investment. Quality quarterly-paying stocks or covered call strategies may outperform.
- **Small sample size:** Even 5 years may be too short for long-term investment decisions.

## Summary
This video is a comprehensive backtest of all 44 currently available monthly dividend stocks, analyzing both dividend income and total portfolio return over 1-year and 5-year periods using a $10,000 DRIP investment. The key finding is that high dividend yield does NOT reliably predict strong total returns — several stocks yielding 15%+ (like ARMOUR Residential and Orchid Island) were among the worst performers, while consistent performers like Gladstone Investment showed strong results across all metrics and time periods. The speaker's conclusion is that monthly dividend stocks should be a starting point for research, not an automatic buy, and investors should consider whether quality quarterly-paying stocks or alternative income strategies (like covered calls) might better serve their goals.
